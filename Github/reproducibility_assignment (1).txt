1 Introduction
In the field of scientific research and knowledge discovery , reproducibility stands as a corner-
stone principle, ensuring the reliability and integrity of findings. The ability to reproduce exper-
imental results and validate conclusions is fundamental for advancing scientific understanding
and fostering trust within the scientific community and beyond. Reproducibility not only serves
as a measure of the robustness of scientific claims, but also plays an important role in promoting
transparency and accountability in research practices.
In recent years, concerns have been raised about the reproducibility crisis across various
disciplines. Studies have shown that a significant portion of published research findings cannot
be reproduced, casting doubt on the reliability of scientific knowledge. This crisis not only
undermines scientific progress but also erodes public trust in the scientific enterprise. While
this is true primarily in the “soft sciences” and humanities, this phenomenon has also shown to
be prevalent in artificial intelligence research.

3 Paper Overview
The paper addresses the problem of goal recognition: given a planning domain, a set of candi-
date goals, and a sequence of observed agent actions, determine which goal the agent is most
likely pursuing. The key insight is that this can be solved using classical planners as black-box
subroutines, without requiring a library of pre-compiled plans.
For each candidate goal, the method computes the cost of the cheapest plan that iscompliant
with the observations versus the cheapest plan that isnon-compliant. The cost difference is used
to define a Boltzmann-style likelihood, which is combined with a prior via Bayes’ rule to produce
a posterior probability distribution over goals.
This approach integrates ideas from several areas covered in AIMA: planning and search
(Chapters 10–11), probabilistic reasoning and Bayesian inference (Chapters 12–13), and the
rational agent framework (Chapter 2).
4 T ask Description
Your aim is to replicate the experiments described in the paper. The goal is to assess whether
the experiments are reproducible, and to determine whether the conclusions of the paper are
supported by your findings. Your results can be either positive (i.e. confirm reproducibility),
or negative (i.e. explain what you were unable to reproduce, and potentially explain why).
Essentially , think of your role as an inspector verifying the validity of the experimental results
and conclusions of the paper.
What Y ou Will Implement
Your implementation will involve:
1. A PDDL compiler that takes an original planning domain and problem, together with an
observation sequence, and produces the modified compliant ( G + O) and non-compliant
(G + O) planning problems as described in Definition 2 and Proposition 3 of the paper.
2. Integration with an existing classical planner to solve the compiled problems. Fast
Downward is recommended ( https://www.fast-downward.org/), but you may use any
suitable planner. You areexpected to use an existing planner — you do not need to imple-
ment one from scratch.
3. The Bayesian scoring layer that computes the cost difference ∆(G, O), the likelihoods,
and the posterior goal probabilities from the planner’s cost outputs.
4. An evaluation pipeline that generates observation sequences (by sampling prefixes of
plans for a hidden goal), runs the recognition process across all candidate goals, and
measures accuracy .
Your glue code should be written in Python, though you will naturally also be writing PDDL
and invoking external tools.

Handling Ambiguity
Where the paper is ambiguous or underspecified, you should document the ambiguity , justify
your interpretation, and note how your choice might affect results. This is one of the most
valuable aspects of a reproducibility exercise. For example, the paper does not specify the value
of the constant β used in the experiments. This is something you will need to determine or
investigate.
Statistical Rigour
Your experiments should include multiple runs where applicable, and results should be reported
with appropriate measures of variance (e.g. standard deviations, confidence intervals). Raw
single-run numbers without any indication of variability are not sufficient.
5 Expected Report Structure
Your report should follow the provided template and include the following sections:
1. Reproducibility Summary (first page) — A one-page summary covering scope, method-
ology , results, and what was easy/difficult.
2. Introduction — Brief context placing the paper in its high-level setting. Keep this short;
your job is to reproduce the work, not to motivate it.
3. Scope of Reproducibility — Which specific claims from the paper are you testing? State
these as concrete, testable hypotheses (e.g. “the method achievesQ ≥ 0.9 on Block Words
at 50% observation”). Each claim should be something your experiments can support or
reject.
4. Background — In your own words, summarise the main ideas behind the paper’s method.
5. Methodology — What did you implement, what design choices did you make, and where
did you deviate from the original? Describe your PDDL compilation approach, your plan-
ner integration, your experimental setup, and computational requirements. Document
any ambiguities you encountered and how you resolved them.
6. Results — Figures and tables comparing your results to the original paper’s. Present
results for each claim identified in the Scope of Reproducibility , and include any additional
experiments needed to fill gaps in the original paper’s specification.
7. Discussion — Do your results support the original claims? What was easy and what was
difficult to reproduce, and why?
A “negative result” which doesn’t support the main claims of the original paper is still valuable
(often more so than a simple confirmation). Generally , a report should include any information
future researchers or practitioners would find useful for reproducing or building upon the paper.
